Wall Street banks are starting to quantify what has quietly become a sizable exposure to private credit, with combined figures now topping at least $100 billion, a development that could draw closer investor attention as concerns build around credit quality and the potential impact of artificial intelligence on certain borrowers. Wells Fargo & Co. (NYSE:WFC) reported $36.2 billion of exposure in the first quarter, while Citigroup Inc. disclosed $22 billion in the fourth quarter and noted zero losses over the life of that portfolio. JPMorgan Chase & Co. (NYSE:JPM) put its exposure at $50 billion, with CEO Jamie Dimon indicating the bank is not particularly concerned, though he acknowledged that stress could emerge under more severe loss scenarios. The underlying composition of these portfolios suggests banks are leaning on structural protections even as scrutiny intensifies, particularly in sectors that could be more exposed to AI-driven disruption such as software. At Wells Fargo, business services, software, and health-care account for roughly half of the collateral backing these loans, with software alone making up 17%. The bank said that in more than 98% of transactions it retains the ability to adjust margins if credit performance weakens, and that loan structures typically include a roughly 40% cushion before losses would be recognized. Within its broader $210.2 billion exposure to non-bank financial firms, about $8 billion is tied to business-development companies, a segment that has seen pressure recently amid valuation concerns and rising redemption requests linked to firms including Blue Owl Capital Inc. and Apollo Global Management Inc. (NYSE:APO). Across the group, banks are pointing to relatively limited direct risk to more volatile corners of private credit while still highlighting areas of growth. Citigroup (NYSE:C) said less than 1% of its loans to non-bank financial institutions are tied to BDCs, within a broader $118 billion portfolio, while Wells Fargo noted that lending to asset managers and funds, including subscription lines backed by investor commitments, rose 42% in 2025 with only $1 million of non-accrual loans recorded. At the same time, Wells Fargo reported that total non-accrual loans to non-bank borrowers increased to $245 million last year from $24 million in 2024, a shift that could suggest early signs of pressure even as overall portfolio protections remain in place and banks continue to frame the risk as manageable.
