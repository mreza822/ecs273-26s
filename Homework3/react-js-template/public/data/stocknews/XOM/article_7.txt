Exxon Mobil, trading at $149.24, sits at the center of this renewed focus on U.S. supply reliability. The stock is up 21.7% year to date and 49.7% over the past year, reflecting investor interest in its scale and exposure to higher oil prices. Over three and five years, total returns of 41.3% and 226.9% show how the company has remained a core energy holding for many portfolios. The Hormuz blockade now adds a new layer to the story. Exxon’s export optionality and global footprint are taking on greater weight for investors who prioritize supply security. The Hormuz blockade is not only reshaping tanker routes, it is also shifting investor attention toward companies that can supply barrels without passing through the Gulf. For Exxon Mobil, higher realized prices and stronger export demand are landing just as shareholders become more vocal about governance and voting power. Recent proposals to separate the Chair and CEO roles and to broaden retail voting options suggest some investors want a clearer balance of power at the top as the company’s market influence grows. The jump in oil prices and tighter supply fit with the view that constrained global investment and supply security concerns can support Exxon's cash generation from core assets such as the Permian and Guyana. Calls for governance changes and closer scrutiny of how voting programs work may challenge assumptions that management will have a free hand in setting capital return and low carbon investment priorities. The geopolitical premium on U.S. barrels, and Exxon's role in supplying six nines purity helium to chipmakers, add dimensions of energy and materials security that are not fully captured in a simple production growth storyline.
