A soon-to-be graduate is thinking ahead. Instead of buying a traditional starter home, they’re aiming for a multifamily property with two to four units. The plan is to live in one unit, rent out the others, and eventually move out while keeping the property as a full rental. It’s a strategy often called “house hacking,” and it’s getting a lot of attention from younger buyers trying to break into real estate. As one commenter summed it up in a recent Reddit thread discussing the young person’s plan, “House hacking with a multi-family property right out of college is one of the best ways to build wealth early.” Don't Miss: The AI Marketing Platform Backed by Insiders from Google, Meta, and Amazon — Invest at $0.91/Share Skip the Landlord Headaches — Invest in Midwest Real Estate Passively The idea sounds ideal on paper. Rental income helps cover the mortgage, and over time, the owner builds equity while keeping living costs low. But those who’ve done it say the reality is a bit more nuanced. One investor who recently bought a triplex shared their experience. “Most of my mortgage is paid for, and after utilities, maintenance, and capex, I’m spending about $1,735/month out of pocket on average,” they said and added that while it’s not fully covered, it’s still “in line with the cost to rent a 1-bedroom” in their city. That theme shows up repeatedly. Many investors said you probably won’t live for free at first, especially with today’s interest rates. Instead, the goal is to live more cheaply while owning an asset. Financing is one of the first hurdles. While the Federal Housing Administration loans are often promoted for low down payments, they can be difficult to use for multi-family properties. “FHA won’t let you have negative cash flow.” That means the property must essentially pay for itself upfront, which is hard to find in many markets. Conventional loans, even with slightly higher down payments, are often more flexible. They allow buyers to qualify based on their income, even if the property doesn’t fully cover its costs early on. Maintenance is another major factor that first-time buyers often underestimate. “[Do not] let realtors convince you that your maintenance and capex is going to be lower than 15% per year,” one experienced investor warned. Capex, short for capital expenditures, refers to big-ticket items like replacing roofs, heating, ventilation, and air conditioning systems, or appliances. These costs don’t show up immediately, but can add up quickly. Several shared stories of starting with a small multi-family property and slowly building a portfolio. One investor said they lived in a three-family home for 15 years before moving out. Today, the property is fully paid off and has tripled in value. Another started with a duplex and gradually expanded. “Bought our first duplex in 2016 with saved student loan money – paid $125K for a tiny little thing,” they said. “One side paid 75% of the mortgage, kept saving the money we would have had to pay for rent and always just bought another.” The approach is often described as “rinse and repeat.” Live in the property, save money, refinance when possible, and move on to the next deal. For many, the biggest benefit isn’t immediate cash flow, but the ability to reduce housing costs while building long-term wealth. “It’s a strong idea and should work well as long as you do your due diligence,” one investor concluded. While house hacking can be an effective way to build wealth, it also comes with financing hurdles, ongoing maintenance and the responsibility of managing tenants.
