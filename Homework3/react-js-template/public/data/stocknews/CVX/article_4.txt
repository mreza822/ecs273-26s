BP Plc (NYSE:BP) indicated that first-quarter performance may be shaped by a sharp split between trading strength and operational softness, as geopolitical tensions in the Middle East reshaped energy markets. The company said its oil trading division delivered an exceptional result, benefiting from elevated volatility after the Iran war disrupted flows through the Strait of Hormuz and pushed prices for crude, gas, and refined fuels higher. Brent crude is up more than 60% this year, creating conditions that could favor global trading desks, with BP's update aligning with a similar signal from Shell. At the same time, US peers Exxon (NYSE:XOM) and Chevron (NYSE:CVX) reported about $7 billion in combined mark-to-market derivative losses tied to hedging positions on cargoes still in transit, suggesting that positioning across the sector could be driving very different outcomes. Despite the trading upside, BP pointed to underlying factors that may weigh on near-term earnings timing. The company said oil production declined slightly compared with the fourth quarter, while natural gas trading came in at average levels. Barclays analyst Lydia Rainforth lowered her first-quarter operating profit estimate, noting that a lag in passing through higher prices means some of the benefit could shift into the second quarter, even as downstream performance improved. Refining margins increased by $1.70 per barrel from the prior quarter, implying a potential uplift of roughly $935 million based on BP's guidance that each $1 increase adds $550 million to pretax operating profit in 2026. Meanwhile, disruptions across Gulf producers forced some halts in oil, gas, and refined product output, adding another layer of uncertainty to supply conditions. Balance sheet trends are also moving in the opposite direction of the company's longer-term targets. BP expects net debt to rise to a range of $25 billion to $27 billion, up from $22.2 billion at the end of 2025, driven largely by a working-capital build of $4 billion to $7 billion linked to the higher price environment. That shift could complicate plans to bring net debt down to $14 billion to $18 billion by the end of 2027, particularly after the company halted share buybacks and withdrew its previous guidance of returning 30% to 40% of operating cash flow to shareholders. With Chief Executive Officer Meg O'Neill newly in place and focused on streamlining the business and prioritizing oil and gas growth, BP's near-term trajectory may depend on whether elevated prices continue to support trading gains while operational performance begins to stabilize.
