Johnson & Johnson (NYSE:JNJ) delivered a first-quarter update that may shape expectations across the pharmaceutical sector, reporting adjusted earnings of $2.70 per share, one cent above estimates, alongside revenue that also came in ahead of expectations. The company moved early to raise its full-year outlook, now guiding for 2026 sales between $100.3 billion and $101.3 billion and adjusted earnings per share of $11.45 to $11.65. Shares were little changed at the open in New York, but the tone of the release could suggest that large drugmakers are continuing to navigate pricing pressure tied to agreements with the White House while maintaining underlying growth. Management described the quarter as a front-loaded investment phase, with Chief Financial Officer Joseph Wolk indicating that spending is being directed toward newer products such as Tremfya and Caplyta. Early signals from Icotyde, a newly approved psoriasis pill, appear encouraging, with around 1,500 patients already on therapy and internal expectations that the drug could evolve into a major contributor over time. The company also indicated it may not be directly targeted by the latest pharmaceutical tariffs following agreements with the Trump administration, while continuing to expand US manufacturing, where $12 billion has already been invested out of a broader $55 billion commitment. Growth trends across the portfolio continue to shift toward newer therapies as legacy products face pressure. Total sales rose nearly 10% year over year despite declining revenue from Stelara due to lower-cost competition. Oncology remains a key driver, with Darzalex contributing strongly and the combination of Rybrevant and Lazcluze growing almost 83% in the quarter, albeit from a smaller base. Outside oncology, Spravato sales increased more than 46%, reflecting demand in treatment-resistant depression. The medical devices segment also grew 7.7%, and with plans to separate the orthopedics unit later this year, the company could be positioning for greater strategic flexibility, potentially opening the door to additional deal activity.
